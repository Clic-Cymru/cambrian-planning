### Page Copy

**H1:** Barn Conversions in Wales: Why 'Class Q' Doesn't Apply
**Context:** Converting a redundant agricultural barn into a stunning residential home is a dream for many developers and self-builders. However, if you are researching this online, you will inevitably encounter a deluge of articles about "Class Q" permitted development. It is vital to understand that planning law is devolved in the UK, and the rules in Wales are entirely different from those in England.

**H2:** The Problem: The 'Permitted Development' Myth in Wales
**Problem:** Many landowners assume they can use 'Class Q' permitted development rights to bypass the need for full planning permission, saving time and money. However, Class Q is an English law (under the Town and Country Planning (General Permitted Development) (England) Order 2015). **In Wales, there is no equivalent permitted development right for converting an agricultural building into a residential dwelling.** Relying on English planning advice for a Welsh property will lead to immediate enforcement action by your Local Planning Authority. In Wales, you must apply for full planning permission.

**H2:** Our Resolution: The Welsh Path to Barn Conversions
**Resolution:** Securing full planning permission for a barn conversion in Wales is entirely possible, but it requires a robust, policy-led approach tailored to the strict requirements of Planning Policy Wales (PPW) and your local authority's Development Plan. At Cambrian Planning, we guide you through the specific Welsh requirements:

1.  **Structural Integrity:** The golden rule in Wales is conversion, not reconstruction. We must prove, usually via a detailed structural survey, that the barn is capable of conversion without requiring major rebuilding. If it's essentially a ruin, it will be treated as a new build in the open countryside and refused.
2.  **Ecological Constraints:** Rural buildings in Wales are highly likely to house protected species, such as bats or barn owls. We coordinate the necessary ecological surveys (which must be timed correctly) to ensure your application isn't stalled.
3.  **Design and Character:** The design must respect the traditional rural character of the building. Inserting excessively large domestic windows or altering the roofline too drastically will be resisted. We work with architects to ensure the design is sympathetic.

**H3:** What about TAN 6 and Rural Enterprise Dwellings?
It's important to distinguish between converting an existing barn into an open-market home and building a *new* dwelling for an agricultural worker. If you need a home on your farm to manage the business, you may qualify under Technical Advice Note 6 (TAN 6). This is a separate, highly complex process that we also specialise in.

**H2:** Why You Need a Welsh Planning Specialist
Applying English planning logic to a Welsh planning authority is a guaranteed recipe for refusal. The emphasis in Wales on sustainable development, landscape protection, and local character requires deep, regional expertise.

## 2. How to Remove an Agricultural Tie in Wales (`/insights/how-to-remove-an-agricultural-tie-in-wales`)

### Page Copy

**H1:** How to Remove an Agricultural Tie (Occupancy Condition) in Wales
**Context:** An Agricultural Occupancy Condition (AOC), commonly known as an agricultural tie, is a planning condition attached to a rural dwelling. It legally restricts the occupancy of the property to a person solely or mainly employed, or last employed, in the locality in agriculture or in forestry. While these conditions serve a purpose, they frequently become obsolete as the agricultural sector evolves.

**H2:** The Problem: The Financial Burden of an Agricultural Tie
**Problem:** If you own a property with an AOC, you are severely restricted. These properties are typically valued 20% to 30% below equivalent open-market houses. Furthermore, securing a mortgage on a tied property is incredibly difficult because lenders view the restricted market as a major risk. Finding a buyer who meets the strict occupational criteria can take years. For many families, the property is unsellable, leaving capital trapped.

**H2:** Our Resolution: The Two Pathways to Removing the Tie
**Resolution:** Removing an agricultural tie is complex, but entirely achievable with the correct legal and planning strategy. At Cambrian Planning, we assess your specific circumstances to pursue one of two proven legal pathways:

**1. The Certificate of Lawfulness (CLEUD)**
If the occupancy condition has been continuously breached for a period of 10 years or more, the condition becomes unenforceable. A breach occurs if the property has been occupied by someone who does *not* work in agriculture for a continuous 10-year period.
*   **The Process:** We compile an airtight legal case, gathering evidence such as sworn statutory declarations, council tax records, employment histories, and utility bills. We then submit an application for a Certificate of Lawful Existing Use or Development (CLEUD). If successful, the tie is legally nullified.

**2. The Section 73 Planning Application (The Marketing Exercise)**
If you cannot prove a continuous 10-year breach, we must apply to the council to remove the condition under Section 73 of the Town and Country Planning Act. To succeed, we must prove there is no longer a long-term demand for agricultural dwellings in the locality.
*   **The Process:** This requires a rigorous, council-approved marketing campaign, typically lasting between 12 and 18 months. The property must be actively marketed at a price that accurately reflects the tie (the 20-30% discount). We manage this entire process alongside specialist rural estate agents, documenting every enquiry and viewing to prove to the council that no eligible buyer exists.

**H2:** Why You Shouldn't Do This Alone
Attempting to lift a tie without specialist advice is highly risky. If you try for a CLEUD and fail to provide sufficient evidence, you not only waste money but you alert the council to the breach, potentially triggering enforcement action. If you run a marketing campaign that doesn't exactly match the council's strict criteria, they will reject it, meaning you have wasted 12-18 months.
