## 2. Holiday Let Planning Permission (`/services/holiday-let-planning-permission`)

### SEO Metadata

- **Meta Description:** Expert planning consultants specialising in holiday let planning permissions in Wales. We navigate Article 4 directions, Section 106 agreements, and changes of use.
- **Target Keywords:** Holiday let planning permission Wales, Article 4 direction Gwynedd, change of use to holiday let, Section 106 restrictions.

### Page Copy

**H1:** Holiday Let Planning Permission Experts
**Context:** The landscape for short-term holiday accommodation in Wales has changed drastically. With the introduction of Article 4 directions in areas like Gwynedd and Eryri (Snowdonia), Local Planning Authorities are exercising unprecedented control over the conversion of residential dwellings into holiday lets, citing pressures on local housing stock.

**H2:** The Problem: Navigating Strict Holiday Let Regulations
**Problem:** If you own a property in an Article 4 area, your permitted development rights have likely been removed. This means you must obtain full planning permission for a 'material change of use' to operate a holiday let. Furthermore, many existing holiday lets are burdened by restrictive Section 106 agreements that prevent permanent residency, tying up capital and limiting the property's value. Attempting to navigate these strict policies without expert representation often ends in refusal or enforcement action.

**H2:** Our Resolution: Securing Your Holiday Let Investment
**Resolution:** We specialise in securing the future of your holiday let business. Whether you are applying for a new change of use, defending against an enforcement notice for an unauthorised let, or seeking to overturn restrictive planning conditions, we build robust, policy-compliant cases. We manage the complex negotiations with the council so you don't have to.

**H3:** Do I Need Planning Permission for a Holiday Let in Wales?
If your property is located within an area governed by an Article 4 direction (such as Gwynedd), yes—you require full planning permission to change its use from a primary residence to a holiday let. Outside of these areas, it depends on whether the change is considered 'material' by the local authority. We can assess your specific situation and advise accordingly.

**H3:** What is a Section 106 Agreement for Holiday Lets?
Local councils frequently use Section 106 legal agreements to restrict the occupancy of holiday lets, ensuring they cannot be used as permanent, unrestricted dwellings. If these conditions are no longer viable or reasonable, we can apply to have them varied or discharged entirely.

## 3. Agricultural & Forestry Tie Removal (`/services/agricultural-forestry-tie-removal`)

### SEO Metadata

- **Meta Description:** Specialist town planners for the removal of Agricultural Occupancy Conditions (AOCs) in Wales. We manage CLEUD applications and Section 73 marketing exercises.
- **Target Keywords:** Remove agricultural tie Wales, Agricultural Occupancy Condition removal, lift agricultural tie, CLEUD agricultural tie.

### Page Copy

**H1:** Experts in the Removal of Agricultural and Forestry Ties
**Context:** Agricultural Occupancy Conditions (AOCs), commonly known as agricultural ties, were traditionally placed on rural dwellings to ensure affordable housing remained available for local farm or forestry workers. While necessary at the time of construction, changes in the agricultural economy mean many of these properties no longer serve their original purpose.

**H2:** The Problem: The Financial Burden of an Agricultural Tie
**Problem:** Properties burdened by an agricultural tie are severely restricted. They are typically valued 20% to 30% below the open market rate. Securing a mortgage on a tied property is notoriously difficult, and finding a buyer who legally meets the strict occupational criteria can take years. For many landowners and inheritors, the property becomes a financial trap rather than an asset.

**H2:** Our Resolution: Two Proven Pathways to Lifting the Tie
**Resolution:** We provide the expertise required to legally remove these restrictive conditions, restoring your property to its full open-market value. We pursue two primary, proven pathways depending on your circumstances:

1.  **Certificate of Lawfulness (CLEUD):** If the occupancy condition has been continuously breached (i.e., occupied by someone not employed in agriculture) for a period exceeding 10 years, we can compile the necessary legal evidence to secure a CLEUD, rendering the tie unenforceable.
2.  **Section 73 Planning Application:** If the 10-year rule does not apply, we manage a highly structured, policy-compliant marketing campaign (typically 12-18 months) to categorically prove to the Local Planning Authority that there is no longer a demand for the dwelling among eligible agricultural workers in the locality.

**H3:** Why You Shouldn't Attempt This Alone
Lifting an AOC is one of the most complex areas of rural planning. Running an invalid marketing campaign that doesn't meet the council's exact specifications will result in a refusal, meaning 12 months of wasted effort. Alternatively, incorrectly attempting a CLEUD can trigger enforcement action. We manage the entire process to protect your asset.

**CTA:** Trapped by an agricultural tie? Let our rural planning specialists assess your options. Request a free case review today.

## 4. Rural Enterprise Dwellings (TAN 6) (`/services/rural-enterprise-dwellings-tan-6`)

### SEO Metadata
- **Title Tag:** Rural Enterprise Dwellings Wales | TAN 6 Experts | Cambrian Planning
- **Meta Description:** Expert planning consultants for TAN 6 Rural Enterprise Dwellings in Wales. We build robust financial and functional cases for agricultural and forestry workers.
- **Target Keywords:** Rural enterprise dwelling Wales, TAN 6 planning permission, agricultural worker dwelling Wales, build house on farm Wales.

### Page Copy

**H1:** Securing Planning Permission for Rural Enterprise Dwellings (TAN 6)
**Context:** Welsh planning policy generally prohibits the construction of new residential dwellings in the open countryside. However, Technical Advice Note 6 (TAN 6) provides a specific, rigorous exception: allowing new homes to be built if they are deemed essential for the operation of a rural enterprise, such as farming or forestry.

**H2:** The Problem: The High Burden of Proof Required by TAN 6
**Problem:** Securing permission under TAN 6 is exceptionally difficult. Local Planning Authorities view these applications with intense scrutiny to prevent the abuse of countryside protections. You cannot simply state you need to live on the land; you must categorically prove a 'functional need' and demonstrate that the enterprise is financially viable and sustainable. Applications lacking meticulous financial and operational evidence are routinely refused.

**H2:** Our Resolution: Building Unassailable Cases for Rural Workers
**Resolution:** We specialise in constructing robust TAN 6 applications. We work closely with agricultural consultants and accountants to compile the comprehensive appraisals required. We demonstrate the essential need for a worker to be on-site at all times (e.g., for animal welfare) and prove the financial soundness of the enterprise, satisfying the stringent tests set out by the Welsh Government.

**H3:** The Functional and Financial Tests
To succeed under TAN 6, we must prove:
*   **The Functional Test:** That it is essential for the proper functioning of the enterprise for one or more workers to be readily available at most times.
*   **The Financial Test:** That the rural enterprise is economically viable, has been established for at least three years, and has clear prospects of remaining viable.

**H3:** Temporary vs. Permanent Dwellings
If your enterprise is new (less than three years old), we can apply for a temporary dwelling (such as a mobile home or log cabin) for a three-year period, allowing you time to prove the financial viability required for a permanent home.

- **Meta Description:** Specialist heritage planning consultants in North Wales. We manage Listed Building Consents and Heritage Impact Assessments for historic properties.

### Page Copy

**H1:** Listed Building Consent and Heritage Planning Experts
**Context:** North Wales boasts a rich architectural heritage, from medieval townhouses in Conwy to traditional rural estates in Eryri. Protecting this heritage is a priority for Local Planning Authorities and CADW. Owning a listed building or a property within a conservation area comes with significant legal responsibilities regarding its upkeep and alteration.

**H2:** The Problem: Strict Heritage Protections and Criminal Liability
**Problem:** A standard planning application is entirely insufficient when altering a historic asset. Making unauthorised alterations, extensions, or even minor changes (like replacing windows) to a listed building in Wales is a criminal offence, potentially resulting in unlimited fines or imprisonment. Navigating the subjective requirements of Conservation Officers without specialist representation is a major risk to both your project and yourself.

**H2:** Our Resolution: Protecting Your Asset and Achieving Your Vision
**Resolution:** We bridge the gap between your development goals and the strict requirements of heritage legislation. We specialise in preparing meticulous Listed Building Consent applications and comprehensive Heritage Impact Assessments. By deeply understanding the historical significance of your property, we design proposals that respect its character while delivering modern functionality, negotiating pragmatically with Conservation Officers to secure approval.

**H3:** What is a Heritage Impact Assessment (HIA)?
An HIA is a mandatory document in Wales for any application affecting a designated heritage asset. It requires a thorough analysis of the building's historical significance and a detailed justification of how your proposed changes will impact that significance. We produce robust HIAs that satisfy both local authorities and CADW.

**H3:** Do I need planning permission AND Listed Building Consent?
In many cases, yes. An extension, for example, will require both full planning permission (for the development itself) and Listed Building Consent (for the physical alteration to the historic fabric). We manage both applications concurrently to ensure a streamlined process.
