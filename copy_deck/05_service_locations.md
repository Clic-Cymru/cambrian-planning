**H2:** The Problem: Strict Heritage Protections and Criminal Liability
**Problem:** A standard planning application is not enough when dealing with historic assets. Making unauthorised alterations to a listed building in Wales—even internal changes or seemingly minor updates like replacing windows—is a criminal offence. Securing Listed Building Consent from Conwy County Borough Council and CADW requires highly detailed, specialised applications. Attempting this without a deep understanding of heritage legislation often results in costly refusals and potential enforcement action.

**H2:** Our Resolution: Securing Your Heritage Development

**H3:** What is a Heritage Impact Assessment?
A Heritage Impact Assessment (HIA) is a mandatory document in Wales for any application requiring Listed Building Consent. It requires a thorough analysis of the building's historical significance and a detailed justification of how your proposed changes will impact that significance. We draft robust HIAs that speak the language of Conservation Officers.

**H3:** Do I need planning permission AND listed building consent in Conwy?
In many cases, yes. Building an extension, for example, will require both full planning permission (for the development) and Listed Building Consent (for the physical alteration to the historic fabric). We manage both applications concurrently to ensure a streamlined process through Conwy Council.

### Page Copy

**H1:** Holiday Let Planning Experts in Gwynedd
**Context:** The tourism economy is vital to Gwynedd, but the pressure on local housing has led to unprecedented regulatory changes. The introduction of an Article 4 direction across the Gwynedd Local Planning Authority area (excluding Eryri, which has its own rules) has fundamentally changed the rules for property owners looking to operate short-term holiday accommodation.

**H2:** The Problem: The Article 4 Direction and Loss of Permitted Development
**Problem:** Because of the Article 4 direction, you no longer have permitted development rights to change the use of a main residence (Class C3) into a holiday let (Class C6) or a second home (Class C5) in Gwynedd. You must now submit a full planning application for a 'material change of use'. Gwynedd Council evaluates these applications against strict criteria in the Joint Local Development Plan, and securing approval is highly complex. Operating without this permission leaves you exposed to immediate enforcement action.

**H2:** Our Resolution: Expert Navigation of Gwynedd's Policies
**Resolution:** Cambrian Planning provides the specialised local knowledge required to secure your holiday let investment in Gwynedd. We construct robust planning applications that address the council's specific concerns regarding housing supply and community impact. If you are facing an enforcement notice for an existing, unauthorised holiday let, we act swiftly to negotiate with the council and submit retrospective applications to legalise your business.

**H3:** Do I need planning permission for an existing holiday let in Gwynedd?
If you were operating the property as a holiday let *before* the Article 4 direction came into force (September 2024), you may not need full planning permission, but you will likely need to prove it through a Certificate of Lawfulness (CLEUD). If you started operating *after* this date without permission, you are in breach of planning control and require retrospective permission.

**H3:** Dealing with Section 106 Agreements
Many existing holiday lets in Gwynedd are burdened by Section 106 legal agreements that strictly limit occupancy. If these conditions are rendering your business unviable, we can apply to the council to have them varied or discharged entirely.

**CTA:** Don't let the Article 4 direction shut down your holiday let business. Contact our Gwynedd experts for an urgent case review.
