### Page Copy

**H1:** Green Infrastructure Statements (PPW 12) in Wales
**Context:** The Welsh Government has fundamentally shifted how development is assessed. Under the latest edition of Planning Policy Wales (PPW 12), Green Infrastructure (GI) is no longer an afterthought; it is a mandatory, core consideration. Almost all planning applications must now demonstrate how they protect, maintain, and proactively enhance green infrastructure.

**H2:** The Problem: Navigating the 'Step-Wise' Approach
**Problem:** Local Planning Authorities across Wales are now routinely refusing or invalidating applications that fail to adequately address Green Infrastructure. Developers are expected to follow a strict 'step-wise' approach (Avoid, Minimise, Mitigate, Compensate) and provide tangible net benefits for biodiversity. Attempting to navigate these new requirements without professional, policy-literate input often leads to costly delays or outright rejection of your scheme.

**H2:** Our Resolution: Comprehensive, Proportionate GI Statements
**Resolution:** We produce robust, highly detailed Green Infrastructure Statements that clearly justify your development. We work seamlessly with ecologists, arboriculturists, and landscape architects to integrate proportionate GI enhancements—such as sustainable drainage systems (SuDS), native planting, or bat roosts—into your design from the very beginning, ensuring a smooth path through planning.

**H3:** Does my application need a Green Infrastructure Statement?
Under PPW 12, a proportionate GI statement is required for almost all forms of development, ranging from minor domestic extensions to major strategic housing sites. The level of detail required scales with the size of the project. We ensure your statement is exactly what the council requires—no more, no less.

**H3:** What is the Step-Wise Approach?
PPW mandates that development must first seek to *avoid* negative impacts on GI. If unavoidable, impacts must be *minimised*. Any remaining impacts must be *mitigated* on-site. Only as an absolute last resort should off-site *compensation* be considered. We structure your application to clearly prove you have followed this hierarchy.

## 7. Design and Access Statements (`/services/design-and-access-statements`)

### Page Copy

**H1:** Compelling Design and Access Statements (DAS)
**Context:** A Design and Access Statement (DAS) is a critical supporting document required for specific types of planning applications in Wales. It is your opportunity to clearly explain the design rationale behind your proposal and demonstrate how it relates to its surrounding context and ensures inclusive access for all.

**H2:** The Problem: Failing to Justify the Design Rationale
**Problem:** A poorly written DAS is often the downfall of an otherwise sound application. If you fail to articulate exactly *why* a building looks the way it does, or how it respects local character and accessibility standards, planning officers are likely to view the design as arbitrary or out of keeping with the area. This is especially true for major developments or sites within sensitive areas like World Heritage Sites.

**H2:** Our Resolution: A Narrative of Exceptional Design
**Resolution:** We draft compelling, highly articulate Design and Access Statements that weave a strong narrative around your project. We break down the design process into clear concepts—amount, layout, scale, landscaping, and appearance—proving to the Local Planning Authority that your development is a thoughtful, high-quality response to its environment.

**H3:** When is a DAS legally required in Wales?
A DAS must accompany a planning application if:
*   It is for a "major development" (e.g., 10 or more dwellings, or a building over 1,000 sqm).
*   It is for development consisting of one or more dwellings, or a building over 100 sqm, located within a designated Conservation Area or a World Heritage Site.

## 8. Retrospective Planning & Enforcement (`/services/retrospective-planning-and-enforcement`)

### SEO Metadata
- **Title Tag:** Retrospective Planning & Enforcement Appeals | Cambrian Planning
- **Meta Description:** Facing a planning enforcement notice in North Wales? We specialise in retrospective planning applications and enforcement appeals to legalise your development.
- **Target Keywords:** Retrospective planning permission Wales, planning enforcement notice appeal, planning enforcement North Wales.

### Page Copy

**H1:** Resolving Enforcement Notices and Retrospective Planning
**Context:** Proceeding with building work or changing the use of a property without the necessary planning permission is a breach of planning control. While not immediately a criminal offence, it leaves you highly vulnerable to enforcement action by the Local Planning Authority.

**H2:** The Problem: The Threat of Demolition and Legal Action
**Problem:** If the council discovers unauthorised development, they can issue an Enforcement Notice. This is a severe legal document requiring you to undo the work—potentially demanding the demolition of an expensive extension or the immediate cessation of a business (like an unauthorised holiday let). Ignoring an Enforcement Notice is a criminal offence. The stress and financial risk associated with enforcement are immense.

**H2:** Our Resolution: Swift, Strategic Legalisation
**Resolution:** We act swiftly to protect your assets. Our first step is often to negotiate with the enforcement officer to pause action while we prepare a robust Retrospective Planning Application. If the council refuses retrospective permission or insists on serving the notice, we possess the expertise to mount a vigorous Enforcement Appeal to Planning and Environment Decisions Wales (PEDW), challenging the notice on legal and planning merit grounds.

**H3:** The 4-Year and 10-Year Rules in Wales
In some cases, if the breach of planning control has existed continuously for a certain period, it becomes immune from enforcement action.
*   **4-Year Rule:** Applies to operational development (building works) and the change of use of a building to a single dwellinghouse.
*   **10-Year Rule:** Applies to all other material changes of use (e.g., agricultural land to a garden) and breaches of planning conditions.
If you meet these criteria, we can apply for a Certificate of Lawfulness instead of retrospective permission.

**CTA:** Received an enforcement warning letter? Do not ignore it. Contact us immediately for urgent, confidential advice.

### Page Copy

**H1:** Expert Representation for Planning Appeals (PEDW)
**Context:** Receiving a refusal notice from your Local Planning Authority is immensely frustrating, but it does not have to be the end of the road. In Wales, you have the statutory right to appeal the council's decision to an independent body: Planning and Environment Decisions Wales (PEDW).

**H2:** The Problem: A Complex, Adversarial Legal Process
**Problem:** The appeals process is fundamentally different from a standard planning application. It is an adversarial, quasi-legal process. You are no longer trying to persuade a local planning officer; you are formally challenging their professional judgement before a government-appointed Planning Inspector. Submitting a weak appeal based on emotion rather than strict planning policy is a guaranteed failure.

**H2:** Our Resolution: Deconstructing the Refusal
**Resolution:** We provide rigorous, objective analysis of the council's reasons for refusal. We deconstruct their arguments and build a powerful, policy-led case to present to the Inspector. Whether through written representations, an informal hearing, or a full public inquiry, we act as your expert advocates, maximising your chances of overturning the refusal.

**H3:** Are there time limits for appealing?
Yes, and they are strict. For householder applications, you typically have 12 weeks from the date of the decision notice to submit an appeal. For most other applications, you have 6 months. If your refusal relates to an enforcement notice, the timeframe can be as short as 28 days.

**H1:** Securing Your Property Rights with Certificates of Lawfulness
**Context:** A Certificate of Lawfulness is a legally binding document issued by the Local Planning Authority. It definitively proves that a specific use of land, a building operation, or a breach of a planning condition is lawful and therefore immune from any future enforcement action.

**H2:** The Problem: Legal Uncertainty and Unsellable Properties
**Problem:** If you have carried out building work or changed the use of a property without permission, you live under the constant threat of enforcement. More commonly, when you attempt to sell the property, the buyer's solicitors will demand proof that the development is legal. Without formal planning permission or a Certificate of Lawfulness, the sale will almost certainly collapse.

**H2:** Our Resolution: Evidentiary Rigour (CLEUD and CLOPUD)
**Resolution:** We act as planning detectives. For existing breaches (CLEUD), we meticulously compile the required evidence—statutory declarations, historical maps, council tax records, and utility bills—to prove beyond doubt that the development has existed for the requisite 4 or 10 years. For proposed developments (CLOPUD), we provide the legal arguments to prove that your planned project falls within permitted development rights and does not require a full planning application.

### Page Copy

**H1:** Navigating Flood Risk and TAN 15 in Wales
**Context:** With extensive coastlines and numerous river valleys, flood risk is a critical planning consideration in North Wales. Development in flood-prone areas is strictly governed by Technical Advice Note 15 (TAN 15). If your site is located within a designated flood zone (Zone B, C1, or C2 on the Development Advice Map), you face significant planning hurdles.

**H2:** The Problem: Immediate Refusal in High-Risk Zones
**Problem:** The Welsh Government takes a highly precautionary approach to flood risk. If you propose highly vulnerable development (like residential housing) in a high-risk flood zone without robust justification, Natural Resources Wales (NRW) will object, and the Local Planning Authority will refuse the application. Without a detailed, technical assessment, your project cannot proceed.

**H2:** Our Resolution: Coordinated Flood Consequences Assessments (FCA)
**Resolution:** We navigate the complexities of TAN 15 on your behalf. While we are not hydrologists, we project manage the entire process. We work closely with specialist flood risk engineers to produce comprehensive Flood Consequences Assessments (FCAs). We then use these technical findings to build a compelling planning argument, demonstrating that the risks can be managed and that the development meets the strict justification tests required by TAN 15.

**H3:** Can I build in a C2 Flood Zone?
Zone C2 indicates an area of the floodplain without significant flood defence infrastructure. Under current TAN 15 policy, highly vulnerable development (like new houses) is generally not permitted in Zone C2. However, less vulnerable commercial developments or changes of use may be acceptable with a rigorous FCA. We provide honest feasibility advice before you spend money on engineering reports.

**CTA:** Is your site affected by flood risk? Contact us to discuss your TAN 15 strategy.
